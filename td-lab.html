My Role
During my internship at TD Lab, I designed a mobile app that teaches newcomers about cultural differences between their home country and Canada. Ultimately, the end goal of the app was to gain more TD customers (i.e. open more TD accounts).
I was the UI/UX designer for the project, creating user flows, mockups, prototypes, and conducting user and usability testing interviews. I worked alongside several developers who coded the app and two business analysts who provided research and insights.
Context and Problem
“Canada is expected to welcome one million new permanent residents over the next 3 years. How might TD Bank develop a new digital experience that incentivizes these residents to make TD Bank their bank of choice?”
TD’s Everyday Banking team presented the lab with the opportunity statement above and asked us to come up with a solution. To scope the upcoming project and ideate a solution, we held a week-long design sprint. During the sprint, we selected the primary users, researched existing competition, and brainstormed potential solutions through discussion and sketching.

Primary Users
Indian newcomers were designated as the primary users of the solution. They were chosen for the following reasons:
  • India represents 12% of all recent immigration to Canada and over 147,000 new residents
  • English is a co-official language in India
  • Indian immigrants are the youngest on average and have the highest amount of immigrants in the 25 - 35 year old range, characteristics of a population that are beginning their banking journey
  • India places a heavy focus on large financial obligations, such as buying/leasing a vehicle or the arrival of a child
Competition
A competitive analysis of the types of products/services the Big Five banks were offering to Canadian newcomers was conducted. Including TD, each bank offered some type of newcomer package for opening an account, with a few banks also offering pre-arrival checklists. Instead of making our users read through another dull checklist, we wanted to present the same type of information in a more unique and engaging way.
First Iteration
Combining several ideas from the design sprint and taking note of competing products/services, the team came up with the following solution: a mobile app that took users step-by-step through the immigration process, ensuring that they didn’t miss anything important. Similar to a guide, the app would assist users with things like completing important documents, estimating expenses, booking flights, and more. In addition to this, the app would provide users with their own TD 'e-card'. Users would be able to earn points by completing steps within the app and once they had arrived in Canada, they could redeem their points for a monetary value by opening an account at a nearby TD branch.

Project Redefinition
With a timeline of four months, we quickly realized that the current scope was too large, especially for a mobile app. Our solution was trying to do too many things (a checklist for both pre- and post-arrival with an incentivization aspect). It seemed unlikely that a mobile app would be enough to satisfy the needs of a user during their entire immigration process; perhaps the biggest transitional and most stressful period a person may experience. Taking a step back and keeping these things in mind, we decided to redefine the project.
Second Iteration
Starting back at square one, we made sure we approached the problem correctly this time around. We conducted user interviews to truly understand the whole immigration process and the problems new immigrants faced.
We discovered that if a user wanted to know something about immigration, they would simply do a quick google search and that the most trustworthy sources were Canadian government websites. In other words, there was no reason to download an app to find information that was easily accessible through a couple of clicks. Not only that, but providing immigration information on an app could pose liability problems. With these new insights, we decided to create a product that was more casual, but still provided relevant and useful information, effectively differentiating us from the rest of the competition.
Enter N2C (Newcomer to Canada), a mobile app that teaches users about the cultural differences between their home country and Canada. Using both the user’s country of origin and their current location, N2C creates a customized experience by providing tailored information for the user. Like the first iteration, N2C would have a rewards system to earn points that could be redeemed for money by opening a TD account.
Cultural Difference?
Bagged milk is common in many parts of Canada. They are usually stored in a pitcher or jug with one of the corners cut off to allow for pouring.
This statement is obvious to Ontarians, but what about a new immigrant? In every other country (and even other provinces and territories in Canada), milk is packaged in cartons, jugs, or bottles. N2C is filled with cultural differences like these, ranging over many topics like Food, Banking, Laws, Housing, and more. It’s easy to google, “What immigration forms do I need to complete?”, but many users don’t know what they don’t know and that isn't easy to search for. Everyday, newcomers experience different pain points related to niche things they haven't learned about, like for example, "How much do I tip? What even is tipping?". The purpose of N2C is to alleviate these daily difficulties, making users' move to Canada easy and welcoming.

Usability Testing
After designing mockups and creating a prototype, it was time to validate the idea. We A/B tested two versions of N2C (TD-branded vs. non-branded) with six end-users, all Indian immigrants who permanently moved to Canada within the last five years.
Here are the key insights we learned from the testing interviews:
  • The most important topics in order were: Laws, Banking, and Housing
  • The least important topics were: Entertainment and Sports
  • Users wanted reccomendations from the app (ex. suggesting nearby places of worship)
  • Users responded positively to the colorful non-branded version, as opposed to the TD-branded version
Overall, user feedback of the app was positive, with several users mentioning that if it existed, they would have downloaded it when they first came to Canada. The majority of users also thought that being able to earn and redeem points for money was a great incentive to invest time into the app, and eventually open a TD account. We heard many personal anecdotes from users regarding specific things that they did not know about, so once the interviews were complete, we knew the concept of N2C showed real value.
Final Iteration
Taking the feedback from the testing interviews, I implemented design changes in the next and final iteration. Here’s a highlight of what was added/changed:
  • Onboarding and authentication
  • Profile
  • A complete rewards system (i.e. more ways to earn points and money!)
  • Edited colours for accessibility purposes
