Revamping Faire's Help Center search and navigation experience

Context
Faire is a wholesale marketplace that connects independent retailers with small brands‚Äîit makes it easy for retailers to discover, purchase, and stock unique products for their stores, while providing brands with a platform to reach a broader audience without the need for a traditional sales force.
At Faire, cross-functional teams are grouped into pillars, which are further divided into pods. I was part of the Support Experience pod, under the Retailer Tools & Programs pillar. The mission of our pod was to help customers with prompt and cost efficient support, and revamping Faire‚Äôs Help Center was the main project I worked on during my internship.
Motivation
The Support Experience pod had a roadmap for several projects to be shipped by the end of H2 2024. Our P0 was an AI support chatbot, while other priorities included a Help Center content audit, support issue taxonomy refresh, and improving and standardizing CSAT collection. With so many upcoming updates to Faire‚Äôs support ecosystem, it felt appropriate to update the Help Center as well, especially since its last update was in 2020 (4+ years ago).
Improving the Help Center could be meaningful not just from a UI/UX standpoint, but also as a lever for reducing Contact Rate (CR%) upstream of the self-serve tree. The Help Center is effective at deflecting a portion of tickets today‚Äîamong users that visit, those who engage (i.e. search, read articles, navigate past user hubs) are 30-65% less likely to enter the self-serve flow and 15-25% less likely to create a self-serve ticket. However, there is also room for improvement‚Äîfor instance, 30% who tried to search or navigate still initiated the self-serve flow.
Current State
Today, the Help Center starts on a parent page, where users must select between the Retailer hub or Brand hub‚Äîeach has content scoped to the specific needs and interests of its audience. Once a user arrives at one of the hubs, they can search, browse by topic, read articles, or enter the self-serve flow through the ‚ÄòContact Us‚Äô button(s) located in the header and sidebar.
UX Audit
To figure out what was and wasn‚Äôt working with the current help center, I conducted a UX audit for both the desktop and mobile experiences.
    Key Insights
  • The hub pages lack strong visual hierarchy‚Äîno particular element stands out as headings, images, buttons, and text all blend together without clear distinction, providing users with no clear direction
  • Across the entire help center, top of page search and navigation elements (header & breadcrumb nav) feel clunky and unintuitive, especially on mobile where the UI becomes cramped
  • There is redundancy and excessive use of space on the search results and topic pages, particularly with article snippets, ‚ÄòRead more‚Äô links, and dividers for every article component
Competitive Analysis
The next step was to figure out best practices when designing a help center. I reviewed several help centers from well-established companies, as well as from Faire's competitors (i.e. other marketplaces). I focused on identifying key elements displaying intuitive navigation, clear categorization, and effective search functionality. Top examples I referred to included Airbnb, Slack, Coinbase, Uber, and Etsy.
    Key Insights
  • No examples used a parent hub. Instead, platforms with multiple user types, like Airbnb and Etsy, started users on the relevant side while offering simple role switching for a seamless transition
  • Many home or hub pages prominently feature a large, central search bar (and related common topics) as a clear CTA, guiding users to find relevant information quickly
  • Many examples highlight search keywords in titles and snippets in the search results page, making it easier for users to find relevant content
Ideation
Taking the UX research into account, as well as reviewing Faire‚Äôs design system for ideal components, I approached the ideation phase broadly, exploring a variety of possibilities to improve the Help Center experience. This included experimenting with layouts for the home/hub, search results, topic, and article pages, as well as designing individual components and refining key navigation components, such as the header and breadcrumb nav. My goal was to address the pain points identified in the research and to align the designs with industry best practices to create a more intuitive and user-friendly experience.
To refine potential designs, I frequently collaborated with CX, engineering, strategy & analytics, brand design, and product designers in the RTP pillar, engaging in regular jam and feedback sessions. With a wide range of explorations, I will focus on the final design outcomes and the key decisions behind them.
Hub pages
To speed up visit duration, we removed the parent hub and now direct unsigned-in users to the Retailer hub‚Äîthe Retailer side was chosen as the default since there are significantly more retailers than brands (~700k vs. ~150k). Important to note that signed-in users will always default to the hub most relevant to them. We also added a hub switcher/toggle at the top of the hero banner for a seamless transition between the Retailer and Brand hubs, since there is no longer a parent hub to complete this action. On non-hub pages, switching between the Retailer and Brand sides is accessed through the chevron located in the header.
Another major change was creating a greater focus on search as the primary CTA‚Äîthis was done by centering and enlarging the search bar, and by adding a greeting (personalized for signed-in users) just above. Regarding the hero banner colors, we consulted with our brand studio team to figure out the best combination. We wanted to use more vibrant colors to generate energy, while at the same time staying within the in-product color palette, especially since the Help Center is a functional surface‚Äîwhich is how we decided on the light orange and purple for the Retailer and Brand sides respectively. The final update was a small QoL improvement: adding topic tags to the articles displayed in the Featured Guides section to enhance clarity, and standardizing previously inconsistent hover states for articles.

Header & breadcrumb nav
The breadcrumb navigation is present on every page, which feels unnecessary, especially on hub pages where users haven't navigated anywhere yet. Some breadcrumbs, like a ‚ÄòSearch‚Äô breadcrumb on the search results page, seem redundant since search is a global function rather than part of a linear navigation. The header is static, looking sparse on desktop but overcrowded on mobile. We‚Äôve updated the header by replacing the Faire logo with a lockup that helps users understand their location within the Faire ecosystem and the Help Center. We also added a language selector and sign-in buttons so users don‚Äôt need to navigate back to other portals for these actions. The header now adjusts based on the user's location‚Äîwhether they‚Äôre on the Retailer or Brand side, the type of page, their scroll depth, and their sign-in status. We simplified the breadcrumb navigation and removed it from some pages to improve clarity.
